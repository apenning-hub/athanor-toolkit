Five domains

Use these as the columns of your own project-governance tracker. An architect's real skill is knowing which domain a problem lives in — and which people can move it.

Contractual

The promises. Who is engaged, by whom, to do what, for how much, bearing which risks.

  • Client–architect agreement
  • Consultant agreements + novation
  • Head contract (e.g. AS 4902 D&C or AS 4000)
  • Energy off-take / heat supply agreements
  • Data centre colocation leases
  • Operator agreements (bathhouse, farm)

Compliance

The rules. Minimum legal standards the built work must demonstrably meet.

  • NCC 2022 Vol One (SA) — Resources 01–04
  • Australian Standards — Resource 06
  • SA Public Health regs (pools)
  • WHS Regulations (plant, confined spaces)
  • Disability Access to Premises Standards
  • Certificate of Occupancy

Planning

The permission. Whether this use, at this scale, in this place, is allowed at all.

  • PDI Act 2016 + Planning & Design Code
  • Southwark Grounds masterplan (Renewal SA)
  • Development approval (Plan SA / SCAP)
  • Heritage — Brew Tower, Copper Kettle
  • Infrastructure agreements + easements
  • Traffic, noise, interface conditions

Good design

The ambition. Everything beyond the minimum: the reason the project deserves to exist.

  • The brief — four simultaneous roles
  • Green Star 5-Star / Communities 6-Star
  • ODASA design review (state projects)
  • PUE / WUE / NABERS targets
  • Universal + climate-adaptive design
  • Civic legibility of infrastructure

Governance

The stewardship. How decisions are made, risks owned and the thing operated for 100 years.

  • Project control group + design gateways
  • Energy utility licensing (ESCOSA / OTR)
  • District heat network ownership model
  • Digital twin + data governance
  • Community reference group
  • Commissioning, soft landings, POE

People map

The architect never works alone. Around them sit five constellations — client, design team, statutory, delivery, operations — each with distinct duties, contracts and moments of power. Click any card for responsibilities.

The Architect

Lead consultant · design author · often contract administrator

Translates the brief into a buildable, approvable, code-compliant proposition; coordinates every consultant into one set of documents; administers the building contract impartially between client and builder; and — uniquely on this project — choreographs infrastructure, civic space and public narrative into a single architectural idea. The architect is rarely the decision-maker; they are the integrator through whom every domain passes.

Relationship key — C: contracted to client · A: engaged/coordinated by architect · S: statutory (owes duty to the law, not the client) · D: delivery contract chain · O: operations & community

Project control

Above every drawing sits a Project Control Group — the governance table where client, developer and advisors make the decisions that bind everyone below. What changes between projects is not the PCG but the delivery structure underneath it: the contract type rewires who the architect answers to, who owns design risk, and when the builder arrives.
