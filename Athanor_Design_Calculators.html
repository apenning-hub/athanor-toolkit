Who drives the egress design

Required aggregate exit width

No point may be more than 20 m from an exit or point of choice, then 40 m max to one of two exits (D2D5); alternative exits 9–45 m apart (D2D6). Assembly rooms >200 m² of Class 9b need doors swinging in the direction of egress.

Exit placement planner — drag exits onto walls or into the core

Reading the plan: green = within 20 m of an exit — D2D5 satisfied directly. amber = beyond 20 m but with a genuine choice of two different directions, reaching an exit within 40 m — legal, but riding on the point-of-choice argument. red = non-compliant: add an exit, move one, or subdivide the compartment. The 9 m minimum separation exists so one fire can't block both exits; the 45 m maximum keeps them sharing the load. Exits don’t have to be in the walls: drag one into the middle and it becomes a fire-isolated core (purple) — a rated stair or passageway whose door IS the exit, exactly how centre-core buildings work. The price is the purple dashed fire-isolated passage it must own all the way to open space. Try the bathhouse at 60 × 40 — then remember pools, not walls, decide where people can actually walk.

What you must verify — and what is only a rule of thumb

Two kinds of numbers live in this toolkit, and your A1 documentation strategy must treat them differently. Code-derived values come from the NCC or an Australian Standard: they are checkable facts — cite the clause, and verify it yourself at ncc.abcb.gov.au before it appears in a submission (clause numbers below are NCC 2022 as adopted in SA; renumbering errors are on you to catch). Rules of thumb have no legal status at all: they are industry planning conventions for getting the sizes roughly right before a consultant exists — name their source, and never present them as compliance.

House rule for submissions: every number in your documentation carries one of two tags — a clause (verified, quoted) or a named convention (attributed, flagged for consultant verification). A number with neither tag is a guess, and juries can smell those.
