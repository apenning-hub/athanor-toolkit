CARBON-POSITIVE BY DESIGN

The Net-Zero
Data Centre

Six decisions stand between a data centre and carbon-positive civic infrastructure: how big, how cooled, how powered, how watered, how built, and how honestly you count. This tool walks through all six in order — every number explains itself, every system comes with a size and a cost, and the ledger at the end tells you the year your building starts paying the planet back.

There is no budget for this project — but there are costs, and you must know them. Systems marked R&D are not excuses; they are design opportunities with dates attached.

Step 0 — Who is this machine for?

People, and their invisible data

Before sizing hardware, size the demand. Every resident carries a digital shadow — cloud photos, streams, feeds, chats with an AI — and every gram of it runs on a server drawing power somewhere. This step turns people into kilowatts, and reveals the studio's quiet punchline: a whole suburb's digital life is tiny next to the brief's data centre. The Athanor is regional infrastructure wearing a neighbourhood's clothes.

Southwark ≈ 3,120 (1,300 dwellings × 2.4) · Adelaide CBD ≈ 25,000 · West Torrens ≈ 64,500

Step 1 — How big is the machine?

Size the computation

Everything downstream — heat, power, water, carbon — scales from one number: the IT load, the electricity the servers themselves consume. Choose it deliberately; the brief's range exists because 1 MW supplies the precinct's heat demand almost exactly.

Inputs

Brief: 1,000–1,500 kW. Below ~750 kW the heat can't carry the precinct in winter; above ~2 MW the summer surplus becomes a liability.
Set automatically by the cooling strategy in Step 2 — drag to override.
Real NEXTDC portfolio: 1.71 (A1 Adelaide) to 4.33 (P1 Perth).
Where do these numbers come from?
  • IT load range — the studio brief (§5a): 1.0–1.5 MW gives 0.9–1.7 MW of continuous recoverable heat against a precinct demand of ~9.1 GWh/yr — near-equilibrium. That balance is the whole architectural argument.
  • kW/rack — dossier §3.1, from NEXTDC's published specs: air-cooled colocation is built at 4–10 kW/rack; hot-aisle containment carries 10–30; liquid-cooled AI racks run 50–140 (one NVIDIA GB200 rack = 120–140 kW). Above ~30–40 kW/rack, air physically can't carry the heat away.
  • kW/m² — dossier §3.3, calculated from published IT capacity ÷ technical space for eleven real Australian facilities. A1 Adelaide = 5 MW ÷ 2,922 m² = 1.71 kW/m².
  • White:grey 1:1 — dossier §5: traditional colocation splits technical floor and support space (UPS, batteries, switchrooms, cooling plant, security, dock) roughly equally.

Step 2 — How is it cooled, and where does the heat go?

Cooling is the heat supply

In this building, cooling is not overhead — it's the product. The cooling strategy sets four numbers at once: PUE (electrical overhead), the share of heat you can recover, the temperature it arrives at, and the water it consumes. Liquid cooling wins on every axis except cost and familiarity.

Cooling strategy

Recovered ≠ used: summer demand collapses. Thermal storage tanks lift this to ~70–80%; borehole seasonal storage (BTES) to ~90%+.
What would have heated the 1,300 homes + bathhouse + greenhouse without you? This choice changes your avoided-carbon claim by 3–4×. Be able to defend it.

The heat cascade — from server to suburb

Where do these numbers come from?
  • PUE by strategy — air ~1.45 (older colocation average), containment ~1.30 (NEXTDC design range 1.25–1.5), direct liquid ~1.15, immersion ~1.08 (vendor + Uptime Institute data; brief targets 1.25–1.35).
  • Recoverable share — air systems capture ~25% usefully (low-grade exhaust air); DLC captures 70–90% in water at 35–65 °C (ASHRAE warm-water classes W3–W5); immersion ~95% at up to 50–60 °C.
  • Utilisation — the Adelaide problem (brief §5f): heat demand collapses December–February. Storage tanks buffer days; BTES banks the summer for winter. Without either, a third of your heat has nowhere to go.
  • Counterfactuals — gas boiler at ~0.185 t CO₂e/MWhth (NGA Factors, natural gas + boiler efficiency); electric heat pump at grid 0.22 ÷ COP 4 ≈ 0.055 t/MWhth. SA has no reticulated-gas mandate for new precincts — arguing 'we displaced gas' needs evidence the precinct would otherwise have used it.
Step 3 — What powers it?

Power: solar first, honesty always

A data centre is a constant load in a state whose grid is already ~75% renewable and heading for 100%. Your levers: on-site solar (roof + carpark canopy), a battery to shift the evening, a renewable PPA for the residual — and the standby question, where diesel's days are numbered.

Inputs

Brief §6: 1,000–2,000 kWp achievable on DC roof + companion roofs + carpark canopy (≈5–7 m²/kWp).
DC roof + companion roofs + carpark canopy (brief §6: ~7,000–8,000 m² is achievable on the precinct).

Where do these numbers come from?
  • Solar yield — Adelaide's 4.83 kWh/m²/day (brief §6) → ≈1.75 MWh/kWp/yr for a well-oriented array. 1,500 kWp needs ~7,000–8,000 m² of roof + canopy.
  • Self-consumption — a data centre is the ideal solar customer: flat, constant load. ~80% of generation is used on site without storage; each MWh of battery shifts ~300 MWh/yr more (daily cycling).
  • PPA — SA commercial power ≈42c/kWh (2026) makes PPAs commercially standard, payback 2–4 years for solar. This is how every serious operator (NEXTDC, AirTrunk, IREN) claims 100% renewable.
  • Standby — diesel N+1 is the incumbent (48 h fuel, NEXTDC practice); HVO is a drop-in ~90% lifecycle-carbon cut; hydrogen PEM fuel cells are in live pilots (Microsoft ran a 3 MW hydrogen backup in 2022); grid-scale BESS standby works where the grid itself is the redundancy — an argument SA's big batteries make plausible.
Step 4 — What does it drink?

Water: near-zero, then circular

Water is the quiet scandal of the industry — evaporative cooling drinks megalitres. Closed-loop liquid cooling takes consumption to near zero; then the building can go further and become a water producer for the precinct: rain capture, pool backwash recycling, condensate recovery.

Inputs

Adelaide ~530 mm/yr × 80% capture efficiency.
Filter backwash is thousands of kL/yr of lightly dirty water — treatable for greenhouse irrigation (brief §9 water cascade).

Annual water balance

Where do these numbers come from?
  • WUE by strategy — dossier water table: traditional evaporative ~2.9 L/kWh (≈32 ML/yr at this scale — 175–200 households' worth); modern hybrid ~1.2; closed-loop liquid ~0.2–0.4; immersion ≈0.05. The brief recommends closed-loop precisely because the cooling loop and the heat network become the same water.
  • Rain — 5,000 m² × 0.53 m × 0.8 ≈ 2.1 ML/yr (brief §7). Store 100–200 kL; use for pool top-up, greenhouse irrigation, cooling make-up.
  • Backwash — public pool filters backwash weekly; a multi-pool facility sends 1,500–3,000 kL/yr to sewer unless treated. A small MBR/UF plant (20–40 m²) recovers most of it for irrigation — SA Health requires the treated stream stays out of the pools themselves.
  • Recycled mains — SA Water's Class A recycled network covers much of inner Adelaide (brief §7) — the correct source for any evaporative trim cooling, not potable water.
Step 5 — What is it made of?

Structure, DfMA + embodied carbon

Operational carbon you can fix later; embodied carbon is spent the day you pour it. The structural typology, the M&E fit-out, and whether the building is designed for disassembly set the carbon debt the operation must repay. This is where the ARCH 5004 documentation package earns its keep.

Inputs

Bolted connections, screw piles, demountable facade cassettes, material passports → end-of-life reuse credit ≈ −15% on fabric embodied.
Generators, UPS, batteries, chillers, CDUs, switchgear — in data centres the services often out-weigh the structure. Modular skids + refurbished switchgear pull this down.

Your whole-building upfront carbon vs published benchmarks

Where do these numbers come from — and what is DfMA really?
  • Fabric intensities — Australian upfront-carbon benchmarks (GBCA 'Embodied Carbon' guidance, NABERS embodied benchmarks, published whole-building LCAs): office/civic RC ~600–800 kg CO₂e/m², steel-framed industrial ~450–650, mass timber ~350–500. A Green Star Buildings submission now requires a 20–40% upfront-carbon reduction against a reference building.
  • M&E embodied — the data centre peculiarity: gensets, UPS, batteries and cooling plant can exceed the structure's footprint. Industry LCAs put DC M&E at 1–2.5 t CO₂e per kW IT. Design responses: modular plant skids (reused between refresh cycles), refurbished switchgear, right-sizing over provisioning.
  • DfMA — Design for Manufacture and Assembly: components engineered off-site (plant skids, precast cassettes, volumetric pods, prefabricated risers), assembled on site with bolted connections. Benefits here: speed (the energy centre can be operational before the bathhouse opens), quality (welds in a factory, not a trench), and disassembly — the building becomes a material bank. Document it with a material passport (e.g. Madaster-style register) in your A2 package.
  • Disassembly credit — designing for deconstruction preserves ~10–20% of fabric carbon value at end-of-life (steel reuse vs recycling). Claimed here as −15% on fabric — cite it as scenario, not certainty.
  • Benchmark sources to cite in your LCA — NABERS Embodied Carbon rating (AU verification scheme, A1–A5); GBCA Green Star 'Upfront Carbon Emissions' credit (−20%/−40% vs reference); RIBA 2030 Climate Challenge v2 (<750 kg/m² offices by 2030); LETI Embodied Carbon Primer (band targets, ~350 by 2030); University of Melbourne EPiC database (AU material coefficients); ICE database (UK coefficients); Infrastructure Australia embodied-carbon projections; published AU office LCAs (693–832 kg/m² upfront BAU). Tools: eTool, One Click LCA.
Step 6 — Count it honestly

The carbon ledger

One chart, thirty years. The building starts life in carbon debt (embodied). Each year it earns credits: avoided heating emissions across the precinct, exported renewable generation — minus whatever grid carbon it still imports. Where the line crosses zero, your building becomes carbon positive. Your job is to drag that crossing left.

Cumulative carbon position, years 0–30

How to defend this claim in a jury
  • Scope it — this ledger covers upfront embodied (A1–A5) + operational energy (B6) + avoided emissions. It excludes refurbishment cycles (B4–B5: server refresh every 4–5 years is real — mention it), water treatment chemistry, and residents' consumption.
  • Avoided emissions are an argument, not a measurement — they depend entirely on the counterfactual. Show both gas and heat-pump scenarios; claim the range, not the best case.
  • The PPA question — market-based accounting lets a PPA zero your residual; location-based accounting doesn't. A rigorous submission shows both numbers. Green Star and Climate Active both accept market-based with additionality tests.
  • Carbon positive ≠ offset shopping — this pathway needs zero purchased offsets. That is the point: the building's own infrastructure does the work. If your numbers only close with offsets, redesign.

Building typologies for a civic machine

Three credible parti diagrams for a 10,000 m² programme where one quarter is sealed machine and three quarters are public life. Each solves the same problem — the data hall's mute, heavy, secure box — differently. Steal one, or argue a fourth.

1 · The Shed + Specials

One big industrial roof (steel, 12–18 m spans) covers the data hall, energy centre and fabrication; the bathhouse, greenhouse and community rooms plug in as 'specials' with their own environmental envelopes. The Victorian pumping-station lineage.

+ Cheapest structure per m² · + DfMA-natural · + future flexibility (hall convertible to market/fab use)
− Risks reading as a warehouse — the civic edge must work hard

2 · The Stacked Hybrid

Data hall and plant form a heavy 2-storey podium (12 kPa floors, thermal mass); the bathhouse sits beside it drawing heat directly; lighter timber community levels stack above the research wing. Section variety becomes the architecture (brief §10).

+ Compact footprint frees the courtyard · + gravity-fed thermal cascade · + timber where timber works
− Vertical services coordination is the studio's hardest drawing set

3 · The Campus of Pavilions

Each programme is its own small building around the courtyard — the data hall a sealed monolith, the greenhouse a glass hall, the bathhouse a masonry bath-block — tied together by the visible district-heating spine as a covered public street.

+ Each envelope tuned to its physics · + staged delivery (DfMA package per pavilion) · + legible energy narrative
− Most facade, most heat loss, most expensive per m²

Design for disassembly — the six layers

Brand's shearing layers, applied: Site (forever) · Structure (60–100 yr — bolted steel, screw piles) · Skin (30 yr — demountable cassettes) · Services (7–15 yr — skid-mounted, exposed, replaceable without touching structure) · Space (5 yr) · Servers (4 yr refresh). A data centre's services churn faster than any building type — design the chase, tray and skid geometry for the third refresh, not the first install.

Document in A2: connection details drawn for un-bolting, a material passport schedule, and a 2050 'second life' drawing — what is this building when the servers leave?

The 12 kPa argument

The data hall floor (12–20 kPa, TIA-942) is 3–5× an office slab. That's embodied carbon — so make it earn a second life: heavy floors accept fabrication, archives, market halls, even pools. Over-capacity is only waste if you never plan to use it.

Dossier §5.1 + §7: the adaptive-reuse questions run both directions — your new building should answer them before an engineer asks.

Grids that let programmes swap

An 8.4 m grid carries racks (14 × 600 mm), carparking (3 bays), lap lanes (2.5 m × 3+), greenhouse gutters (4.2 m bays) and classrooms. One dumb, generous grid beats four clever ones — it is the cheapest disassembly strategy there is.
